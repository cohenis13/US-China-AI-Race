Methodology

How the U.S.–China AI Race Tracker measures each dimension — sources, proxies, and known limitations.

Overview Executive Summary — Score Construction

What this is

The executive summary section at the top of the dashboard aggregates the six dimension outputs into a single comparative scorecard. It is generated automatically by scripts/build_executive_summary.py and stored in data/executive_summary.json, which the frontend loads on every page view. The builder runs as the final step of every dimension refresh workflow, so the summary always reflects the most recent data across all six dimensions.

Normalization approach

Dimensions use one of three methods to produce a 0–10 score:

Count-share (Frontier Models, Talent, Compute)

Raw counts (model updates, paper counts, Rmax PFlop/s) are converted to a U.S. share of the combined U.S. + China total, then scaled to 0–10 and clamped to [0.5, 9.5] to avoid extreme poles when data quality is low: 

us_score  = clamp( (US / (US + China)) × 10, 0.5, 9.5 )
china_score = 10.0 − us_score

By construction, U.S. + China = 10 for these dimensions. The 9.5 ceiling on the Compute score is the primary visible effect of the clamp — the raw TOP500 share would yield ~9.7 for the U.S., but this reflects the TOP500 undercount for China rather than true dominance.

Independent composite / 10 (Adoption, Energy)

Adoption and Energy use 0–100 composite scores (computed independently for each country). The executive summary score is simply: 

score = composite_score / 10

These scores do not sum to 10 — they are independent assessments of each country’s standing on the underlying proxies.

Share-of-combined / 10 (Diffusion)

Diffusion composite scores already sum to 100 (U.S. + China = 100 by construction). The executive summary score is: 

score = composite_score / 10

So U.S. + China ≈ 10 for Diffusion (small rounding differences only).

Score table and radar chart

The six scores populate both the radar chart and the dimension score table. The radar chart order is fixed as: Frontier Models, Compute, Adoption, Diffusion, Energy, Talent — matching the DIMS array in index.html.

Text generation

The “Current read” sentence and four strategic insight bullets are generated programmatically from the scored dimensions. The current read lists dimensions by winner (U.S. vs. China), sorted by margin (largest advantage first). The insights follow a fixed template: (1) strongest U.S. advantage, with a special case for the Compute dimension to flag the TOP500 undercount caveat; (2) strongest China advantage; (3) most closely contested dimension by gap; (4) a standing note on binding structural constraints.

Key caveats

  • Compute score (9.5 US / 0.5 China): Extreme values reflect TOP500 submission gaps, not actual capability parity. China operates exascale-class systems not listed on TOP500. Treat with very low confidence as a relative measure.
  • Frontier Models score: Reflects Hugging Face Hub activity only. China’s closed-model ecosystem (DeepSeek, Qwen API) is materially broader than this captures.
  • Talent score: Paper volume, not quality or headcount. China leads on volume; the U.S. leads on top-cited work and lab concentration.
  • Adoption and Energy scores: Based on curated reference data with annual update cadence — not live signals.

Adoption AI Adoption in the Economy — Multi-Proxy Index

What this measures

A composite index approximating economy-wide AI adoption in the U.S. and China, built from two public proxies: enterprise AI adoption rates (survey-based) and industrial automation density (robot density per manufacturing worker). The composite is a transparent, directional proxy — not a definitive measure of total national AI usage.

Adoption vs. diffusion: This section focuses on adoption — whether and how much firms and institutions are using AI. It does not attempt to fully measure diffusion (how broadly that use spreads across geographies and sectors), which is a distinct and harder-to-measure concept.

Why a multi-proxy composite index?

No single public data source provides a clean, symmetric, and automatable measure of AI adoption across both the U.S. and Chinese economies:

  • Survey data (e.g., McKinsey) has limited China-specific granularity and inconsistent sampling across countries. It is more useful as a directional signal than a precise national figure.
  • Public filing data (e.g., SEC EDGAR) covers Chinese ADRs only — a technology-heavy sample that excludes Tencent, ByteDance, Huawei, and the vast majority of Chinese firms. This created a structurally asymmetric comparison.
  • Hard deployment metrics (e.g., industrial robot density) are highly symmetric and verifiable but measure industrial automation broadly, not AI specifically.

The composite combines these proxies so that their individual weaknesses are partially offset. Weights and normalization are fully documented and transparent.

Proxy 1 — Enterprise AI Adoption Rate (55% weight)

Sources: McKinsey & Company, The State of AI 2025 (published May 2025), and Stanford HAI AI Index 2025 (published April 2025). Supplemented by CAICT (China Academy of Information and Communications Technology) White Paper on China’s AI Development (2024) for the China-specific figure.

Definition: Percentage of organizations reporting that they use AI in at least one business function.

Values used: U.S. (North America): ~72% (McKinsey 2025, corroborated by Stanford AI Index 2025). China: ~70% (estimated from McKinsey 2025 regional data, CAICT 2024, and Stanford AI Index 2025; see coverage note below).

Normalization: Already expressed as a percentage; used directly on the 0–100 scale.

Coverage note — China: The McKinsey survey does not cleanly break out China at the country level. The ~68% figure is an estimate derived from McKinsey’s 2024 global and regional data, cross-referenced with CAICT surveys on Chinese enterprise AI adoption. It should be treated as directional (confidence: medium), not as a precise national figure.

Proxy 2 — Industrial Robot Density (45% weight)

Source: International Federation of Robotics (IFR), World Robotics 2024 annual report (published October 2024, covering 2023 operational data).

Definition: Installed industrial robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers. This is one of the most publicly verifiable and country-comparable measures of automation deployment depth embedded in the physical economy.

Values used: U.S.: 295 robots/10K workers (up from 274 in 2022). China: 470 robots/10K workers (up from 392 in 2022 — China overtook Japan and Germany in density ranking in 2023). For reference: South Korea ~1,012 (global outlier); Germany ~429; Japan ~419.

Normalization: (value ÷ 600) × 100, where 600 robots/10K workers is the normalization reference — above the leading non-outlier economies (Germany ~429, Japan ~419, China ~470) and well below South Korea’s ~1,012. The reference was updated from 500 to 600 when China’s 2023 value (470) crowded the previous ceiling.

Scope note: Robot density measures industrial automation broadly, not AI-specific deployment. It captures physical automation in manufacturing (automotive, electronics, precision assembly) and is an imperfect proxy for AI adoption in services, software, or consumer applications. Its strength is its symmetry and verifiability; its limitation is that it conflates pre-AI robotics with AI-driven automation.

Composite construction

Formula: Composite = 0.55 × enterprise_adoption_score + 0.45 × robot_density_norm_score

If a proxy is unavailable for one country, that proxy is excluded and the remaining proxy is re-weighted to 100% for that country. This prevents a missing proxy from artificially zeroing out one country’s score.

Enterprise adoption is weighted slightly higher (55%) because it more directly measures AI-specific organizational behavior. Robot density (45%) is a more objective and symmetric measure but captures industrial automation broadly.

Update cadence and automation

The script runs weekly via GitHub Actions. The underlying reference data updates annually: McKinsey publishes in approximately May each year; Stanford AI Index in approximately April; IFR publishes in approximately October. When new editions are released, the reference values in scripts/fetch_adoption.py are updated manually and the composite recalculates automatically on the next run.

Next expected updates: IFR World Robotics 2025 (~October 2025, covering 2024 data); McKinsey State of AI 2026 (~May 2026); Stanford AI Index 2026 (~April 2026).

What this composite does NOT capture

  • Consumer AI usage (individuals using AI tools, apps, or devices)
  • AI usage by small and medium enterprises
  • Private or unreported AI deployment
  • AI application quality or depth of integration
  • Software-only AI deployments not captured in industrial robot density
  • Sector-specific adoption in financial services, healthcare, or consumer services
  • The vast majority of Chinese firms (domestic, non-export-listed companies)

Why the previous approach (SEC EDGAR filing disclosure) was replaced

The v1 adoption section used SEC EDGAR full-text search to classify companies based on AI disclosure language in annual filings (10-K for US, 20-F for Chinese ADRs). This approach had a structural flaw: the China sample consisted exclusively of US-listed Chinese ADRs — a technology-heavy subset that excluded Tencent, ByteDance, Huawei, CATL, and most of China’s economy. The comparison was asymmetric by construction. The new composite index uses sources that are either genuinely country-comparable (IFR) or that, while imperfect, apply broadly to both economies (McKinsey global survey).

Diffusion Global AI Diffusion — Two-Proxy Composite

What this measures

How far U.S. and Chinese AI stacks have spread beyond their home markets — the external reach of each country’s AI ecosystem in terms of model adoption by foreign developers and cloud infrastructure accessible to foreign customers.

Diffusion vs. Adoption: This section focuses on diffusion — whether and how far a country’s AI outputs are being absorbed into foreign economies, products, and research pipelines. It does not measure domestic AI usage, which is tracked separately under Adoption.

Both proxies are scored as a share of the combined U.S. + China total, so the two country scores always sum to 100. This framing reflects the competitive nature of diffusion: reach that one country gains is, in relative terms, reach the other does not hold.

Proxy 1 — HF Open-Model Monthly Downloads (55% weight, live)

Source: Hugging Face Hub API (live, daily refresh). Labs and country mappings from data/labs.json — the same curated list used by the Frontier Models dimension.

Definition: Sum of monthly downloads across the top-100 most-downloaded models per tracked lab. Unlike the Frontier Models dimension, there is no date filter — this captures the full active install base, not just recent activity. A model being downloaded globally indicates active embedding in foreign products, pipelines, and research.

Scoring: (country downloads / US + China combined downloads) × 100.

Known undercount — China: Hugging Face Hub is the dominant platform for the global open-source developer community, but Chinese models are also widely distributed through ModelScope, Gitee AI, and other domestic platforms. Downloads via those platforms are not captured here. The China HF share is therefore a lower bound on total Chinese open-model diffusion.

Proxy 2 — Cloud AI Platform International Footprint (45% weight, curated)

Sources: Official provider documentation — AWS Global Infrastructure, Microsoft Azure geographies, Google Cloud locations, Alibaba Cloud global locations, Huawei Cloud regions, Tencent Cloud regions — as of Q1 2026.

Definition: Number of international countries and territories served by each country’s major public cloud providers with AI/ML platform services (combined, deduplicated estimate).

Values used: U.S. (AWS + Azure + Google Cloud): ~190 countries/territories. China (Alibaba Cloud + Huawei Cloud + Tencent Cloud): ~70 countries/territories.

Scoring: (country countries / US + China combined countries) × 100.

Why this proxy: Cloud footprint is the infrastructure layer of AI diffusion. A foreign organization that cannot access a country’s cloud provider cannot easily adopt that country’s AI APIs, services, or managed models. U.S. providers near-saturate the accessible global market; Chinese providers have expanded significantly through Belt and Road Initiative partner countries and enterprise markets in Asia-Pacific, Middle East, and Africa, but remain below U.S. coverage in aggregate.

Scope note: Country count is a measure of reach, not depth. A cloud provider that serves 190 countries may have vastly different revenue, usage, and AI workload depth across those countries. This proxy measures whether access exists, not how much of it is used.

Composite construction

Formula: Composite = 0.55 × hf_download_share + 0.45 × cloud_footprint_share

By construction, US composite + China composite = 100. The HF proxy is weighted higher (55%) because it provides a live, high-frequency signal of actual global model adoption. Cloud footprint (45%) is more structural and slower-moving but captures the infrastructure layer through which AI services are delivered internationally.

Update cadence

The HF downloads proxy is fetched daily via GitHub Actions — HF download figures update continuously and reflect the trailing 30-day window. The cloud footprint proxy is curated and reviewed annually; values in scripts/fetch_diffusion.py are updated manually when providers announce significant geographic expansion.

What this composite does NOT capture

  • Domestic AI adoption within each country’s home market (see Adoption dimension)
  • Chinese model downloads via ModelScope, Gitee AI, or other domestic platforms
  • Closed/proprietary model API usage (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini, Qianwen API)
  • AI hardware exports (chips, servers) as a diffusion vector
  • Influence via joint ventures, technical assistance, or standards bodies
  • Quality or depth of AI integration in foreign deployments
  • Revenue or economic impact of diffusion

Energy Energy & Power Constraints — AI Scaling Capacity

What this measures

How well the U.S. and China can sustain large-scale AI compute growth from an energy and power infrastructure perspective. This is not a measure of total electricity generation or consumption. It focuses specifically on:

  • The pace of electricity supply expansion (can new capacity be built fast enough?)
  • Available headroom in the grid before AI/data center demand becomes a constraint
  • The speed at which new power capacity can actually reach data center operators

A country may have vast total electricity capacity but still face AI-specific energy bottlenecks if grid connection is slow, permitting is blocked, or data center demand is already consuming a large share of available generation.

Proxy 1 — Electricity Capacity Addition Rate (40% weight)

Sources: U.S. EIA, Electric Power Monthly (January 2024); IEA, World Energy Outlook 2024 (October 2024); China National Energy Administration (NEA) 2023 Annual Report.

Definition: Annual additions to installed electricity generation capacity, expressed as a percentage of the existing installed base. Captures the pace of energy supply expansion — the most important long-run factor for sustaining AI compute growth.

Values used: U.S.: ~3.7% (46 GW added in 2023; 1,247 GW installed base). China: ~11.9% (345 GW added in 2023; 2,900 GW installed base — driven by ~217 GW solar, ~75 GW wind; China has led global capacity additions every year since 2013).

Normalization: (rate ÷ 15%) × 100, capped at 100. 15% is above any major economy’s sustained growth rate and provides meaningful spread between U.S. (~25) and China (~79).

Proxy 2 — Data Center Power Demand Headroom (35% weight)

Source: IEA, Energy and AI (January 2025); EIA Electric Power Annual 2023 (US generation total); NBS China Statistical Yearbook 2023 (China generation total).

Definition: Data center electricity consumption as a percentage of total national electricity generation, inverted to a headroom score. Lower data center share = more capacity available for AI scaling = higher score.

Values used: U.S.: ~4.8% DC share (200 TWh / 4,178 TWh) → headroom score 52.0. China: ~2.5% DC share (220 TWh / 8,900 TWh) → headroom score 75.0. China’s lower share reflects its significantly larger generation base.

Normalization: Headroom score = (10 − dc_share%) ÷ 10 × 100. At 10% DC share, headroom = 0 (fully pressured); at 0%, headroom = 100. IEA projects US data center demand could exceed 500 TWh by 2030 under a high-AI scenario, which would push DC share above 10% — making this the binding constraint for US AI scaling by late decade.

Confidence note — China: China’s data center electricity statistics are less granular than US figures. The ~220 TWh estimate from IEA covers ICT workloads broadly; AI-specific demand is a subset. Treat as directional.

Proxy 3 — Grid Connection Speed Score (25% weight)

Sources: Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL), Queued Up: Characteristics of Power Plants Seeking Transmission Interconnection (2024 edition); IEA, Energy and AI (January 2025); China NDRC/NEA.

Definition: A curated 0–100 score reflecting how quickly new electricity generation capacity can be connected to the grid and made available to data center operators. Combines interconnection queue depth, regulatory speed, and state capacity for infrastructure direction.

Values used: U.S.: 30/100 — LBNL 2024 reports 2,600+ GW of generation waiting in US interconnection queues; median wait exceeds 5 years. FERC Order 2023 reforms are in progress but have not yet cleared the backlog. Multiple AI data center projects have been delayed by grid connection timelines. China: 65/100 — State-directed permitting (NDRC) enables faster execution. Score is moderated from higher by: (a) renewable curtailment in some western regions; (b) transmission bottlenecks between western generation and eastern demand centers, partially addressed by Ultra-High-Voltage (UHV) buildout.

Confidence note: Grid connection speed is a curated qualitative- quantitative composite, not a single directly measured metric. Confidence is medium for both countries. The US score may improve if FERC Order 2023 reforms meaningfully reduce interconnection timelines over 2025–2027.

Composite construction

Formula: Composite = 0.40 × capacity_score + 0.35 × headroom_score + 0.25 × grid_score

Capacity addition rate (40%) is weighted highest because it directly measures the rate of energy supply expansion — the most important factor for long-run AI scaling. Headroom (35%) captures near-term grid slack. Grid speed (25%) captures the infrastructure bottleneck that can prevent theoretical capacity from being practically usable.

Update cadence

The script runs weekly via GitHub Actions (Monday 11:00 UTC). Underlying data updates annually: EIA Electric Power Monthly (~January, US capacity additions); IEA World Energy Outlook (~October, China capacity); IEA Energy and AI (irregular, data center demand); LBNL Queued Up (~April–May, US interconnection queue). When new editions are published, update reference values in scripts/fetch_energy.py.

What this composite does NOT capture

  • Total electricity generation or consumption
  • Energy cost (electricity price per kWh, which affects AI ROI)
  • Carbon intensity or renewable mix of AI-relevant power
  • Nuclear power capacity expansion (US SMR pipeline; China nuclear buildout)
  • Private or off-grid power arrangements for specific AI campuses
  • Long-run transmission infrastructure adequacy (UHV lines, HVDC)
  • Water availability constraints for data center cooling

Frontier Models Model Update Activity

Measures public model update activity on Hugging Face Hub from a curated list of tracked AI labs. Counts new model versions published by US-attributed vs China-attributed labs in the rolling 12-month window.

What this does not capture: Closed models (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini Ultra) are not counted. Labs are classified by country of the primary organization.

Compute High-Performance Computing Capacity

Aggregate HPL Rmax benchmark performance (PFlop/s) and system count from the full TOP500 supercomputer list, grouped by country. Updated twice yearly (June and November).

What this does not capture: Private AI clusters, cloud GPU farms, and systems not submitted to TOP500. China operates exascale systems not in TOP500, so its disclosed capacity is likely a significant undercount.

Talent AI Research Output

Volume of AI research papers from US- and China-affiliated institutions, sourced from OpenAlex over the last 12 months. A proxy for research output, not researcher headcount or citation impact. Papers with multinational authorship are counted in each country represented.
