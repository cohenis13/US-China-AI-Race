Methodology

How we measure the U.S.–China AI race  ·  Data sources, classification logic, and limitations

Overview

This tracker measures the U.S.–China AI competition across six dimensions: frontier models, compute, domestic adoption, global diffusion, talent, and energy. The goal is not a single authoritative ranking — it is a structured, transparent, and regularly updated view of publicly observable signals across each dimension.

We prioritize transparency over precision. Every metric includes its source, its classification logic, and an honest assessment of its limitations. When data is uncertain, we say so rather than forcing a clean answer.

Important caveat — v1 scope

The current live metric is a v1 prototype measuring lab activity on Hugging Face Hub. It is a useful proxy, not a comprehensive census of all frontier AI development. See the Frontier Models section below for details.

Dimension 1 — Frontier Models (Live)

What we measure

Public model update activity on Hugging Face Hub from tracked labs in the last 30 days. This is a proxy metric for lab output velocity — not a definitive ranking of frontier model capability. It counts how many models a curated set of known AI labs have updated or published on Hugging Face Hub within a rolling 30-day window.

Why this metric

Hugging Face Hub is the dominant public repository for open and semi-open AI models. Major US and China frontier labs publish or mirror their model weights and updates there. The cadence of updates is a reasonable signal of research activity and output velocity. It is the most consistently available, automatically retrievable public signal for cross-country lab comparison — even though it is an imperfect proxy (see Limitations below).

Data source

Hugging Face Hub API
Endpoint: https://huggingface.co/api/models?author=<org>&sort=lastModified&direction=-1
Updated: daily at 06:00 UTC via GitHub Actions
Coverage: public models only (closed-weight models not included)

Classification logic

Labs are classified by country using a manually maintained mapping file: data/labs.json. Each entry specifies the lab name, country, and one or more Hugging Face author slugs. Four categories are used:

  • US: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google/DeepMind, Meta AI, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Cohere, Allen AI, Stability AI, xAI, Apple, Amazon, Together AI, Salesforce AI
  • China: Alibaba/Qwen, DeepSeek, Zhipu AI/THUDM, 01.AI, Baidu, ByteDance, Shanghai AI Lab, Moonshot AI, MiniMax, Tencent, Huawei/Noah's Ark Lab
  • Other (non-US/China): Identified labs headquartered outside the US and China. Currently tracked: Mistral AI (France). These labs are clearly classifiable but are not part of the US–China bilateral comparison — they are shown separately so they do not distort the primary counts.
  • Unknown: Any lab author not matched in labs.json, or any entry whose country origin is genuinely unclear.

The lab list is intentionally conservative — we only include labs with a clear, established frontier-model or open-model track record. data/labs.json is editable; corrections and additions can be submitted via the project repository.

NVIDIA caveat

Note on NVIDIA's contribution to the US count

NVIDIA frequently accounts for a large share of US model updates under this metric, but its HF publishing activity differs from that of frontier LLM labs in important ways. NVIDIA publishes specialized models across many non-LLM domains (robotics, medical imaging, weather forecasting, protein structure prediction) and also re-hosts some third-party model weights — including China-origin models such as Kimi, Qwen, and GLM — under its own HF account. All of these count toward the US total under this metric's methodology. Readers comparing US and China raw counts should be aware that NVIDIA's volume reflects broad multi-domain publishing, not general-purpose frontier LLM output alone. The lab-by-lab breakdown table on the dashboard allows readers to inspect NVIDIA's contribution and adjust their interpretation accordingly.

Limitations

  • Closed models excluded: Flagship closed-weight models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5/4, Gemini Ultra) do not appear on HF Hub and are not counted. This understates the effective frontier capability of US labs in particular.
  • China undercount: Some China-affiliated labs publish primarily to domestic platforms (ModelScope, WiseModel, etc.) rather than HF Hub. This may understate China's actual model output velocity.
  • Volume ≠ capability: A lab releasing many fine-tuned checkpoints or domain-specific models appears more active than one releasing a single major model per quarter. High counts do not imply greater frontier capability.
  • Rolling window: The 30-day window is rolling, so counts shift each day even without new releases.
  • Lab list scope: Only labs in data/labs.json are tracked. Independent researchers and smaller organizations are not included.

Dimension 2 — Talent (Live)

What we measure

The headline comparison is US vs. China: how many AI-related research papers in the last 12 months have at least one author affiliated with a US institution, and how many have at least one author affiliated with a Chinese institution. This is a proxy for institutional research activity — not a measure of researcher headcount, citation impact, or overall talent quality. OpenAlex is a global index, so rest-of-world data is available and shown as context, but it is not the primary story.

Why this metric

Research publication volume is a consistently available public signal for institutional AI activity. OpenAlex was chosen over arXiv's own API because OpenAlex pre-computes institution-to-country mappings from publisher metadata, yielding far lower unknown rates than keyword matching against raw author strings. OpenAlex indexes papers from most major academic publishers and preprint servers including arXiv, IEEE, ACM, Nature, and others, making it broader in scope. The main chart displays US and China only; rest-of-world totals are reported as a context note.

Data source

OpenAlex API
Endpoint: https://api.openalex.org/works
Filter: concepts.id:C154945302|C119857082|C204321447|C31972630,from_publication_date:YYYY-MM-DD
Concepts: Artificial Intelligence (C154945302), Machine Learning (C119857082), Natural Language Processing (C204321447), Computer Vision (C31972630)
Group by: authorships.institutions.country_code
Updated: daily at 07:00 UTC via GitHub Actions
Coverage: papers indexed by OpenAlex matching the above concept IDs

How country attribution works

OpenAlex links each author to their institution using publisher-provided affiliation metadata, then maps institutions to countries using ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 country codes. This is done at ingest time by OpenAlex — the tracker does not perform any keyword matching.

A single API call with group_by=authorships.institutions.country_code returns the full country breakdown for the 12-month window in one response. The count for each country reflects the number of papers where at least one author has an identified institution in that country.

Attribution is non-exclusive. A paper with both US and Chinese co-authors is counted once in the US total and once in the China total. This means the sum of all country counts can exceed the total paper count. The dashboard shows both total papers (unique papers) and total attributed (sum of all country attributions) to make this visible.

Four summary categories are used:

  • US: Papers where at least one author has an institution in the United States (country code: US)
  • China: Papers where at least one author has an institution in China (country code: CN)
  • Other: Papers attributed to any other country, not counting US or CN attributions
  • Unknown: Papers where no author has any identified institutional affiliation in OpenAlex

In the per-paper table, each paper is assigned a single primary country label based on its full list of author countries:

  • US only (possibly with other non-China countries) → US
  • China only (possibly with other non-US countries) → China
  • Both US and China → Mixed
  • Other countries only → Other
  • No identified affiliations → Unknown

Dashboard framing

The main chart shows US and China only. OpenAlex is a global index and rest-of-world paper counts are substantial (the sum of all other countries exceeds either the US or China totals individually, because it aggregates ~198 countries). Showing Other as a primary bar would dominate the chart and obscure the US–China comparison, which is the purpose of this tracker. Rest-of-world totals are reported as a context note beneath the stat boxes. Unknown is a metadata coverage note, not a headline figure.

Limitations

  • Volume ≠ quality: Paper count does not measure citation impact, benchmark performance, or practical significance. A high count does not imply greater frontier capability.
  • China undercount: Chinese researchers may publish to domestic platforms (CNKI, WiseModel, domestic journals) not well-indexed by OpenAlex. This likely understates China's total research output.
  • Double-counting of collaborations: Papers with multinational author teams are counted in each country represented. US–China collaborations inflate both countries' totals relative to the total paper count.
  • Unknown reflects missing metadata: Papers classified as Unknown have no institutional affiliation recorded in OpenAlex. This can reflect preprints without publisher metadata, independent researchers, or affiliation data not yet ingested by OpenAlex. For concept-tagged AI papers, Unknown rates are typically near zero.
  • Concept classification: OpenAlex assigns concept tags algorithmically. Some AI-adjacent papers may be missed; some tangentially related papers may be included.
  • Coverage lag: OpenAlex may index some papers with a delay after publication, particularly for venues with slower metadata pipelines.

Dimension 3 — Compute (Live)

What we measure

The headline comparison is US vs. China: aggregate HPL benchmark performance (Rmax, in PFlop/s) from the most recent TOP500 supercomputer list. System count is shown as a secondary supporting metric. This is a proxy for disclosed high-end compute infrastructure — not a direct measure of AI training capacity.

Why HPL performance, not system count

System count treats a small GPU cluster the same as an exascale machine. Aggregate Rmax weights each system by its benchmark performance, so a single exascale machine contributes proportionally more than hundreds of smaller systems. This makes it a better proxy for total high-end compute capacity.

Data source

TOP500 — full XML download
URL: https://www.top500.org/lists/top500/YYYY/MM/download/TOP500_YYYYMM_all.xml
Metric: HPL Rmax (High Performance Linpack benchmark). Source unit is GFlop/s; stored as PFlop/s (÷ 1,000,000).
Coverage: all 500 systems in the edition (complete list, not a sample)
Updated: twice yearly (June and November) — script auto-detects the latest edition
Country: as reported by the submitting institution to TOP500 (dedicated XML field, no inference required)

How country attribution works

Country is taken directly from the TOP500 list as reported by the submitting institution. Systems are mapped to four buckets:

  • US: Country = "United States"
  • China: Country = "China"
  • Other: Any other identified country
  • Unknown: Missing country field (rare)

The main chart shows US and China only. Other is reported as a context note.

Limitations

  • Excludes private AI clusters: Large GPU training farms (e.g. internal clusters at major AI labs) are not listed on TOP500 unless voluntarily submitted. This likely understates both US and Chinese AI compute capacity.
  • Disclosed systems only: TOP500 only includes systems voluntarily submitted. Country-level totals reflect disclosed capacity and may understate broader compute infrastructure.
  • Not AI-specific: TOP500 systems include weather forecasting, nuclear simulation, and other HPC workloads — not exclusively AI training. HPL performance does not directly translate to AI training throughput.
  • Snapshot, not continuous: The list is updated twice yearly. Capacity added between editions is not reflected until the next update.
  • HPL ≠ AI performance: The HPL benchmark measures dense linear algebra performance. AI training uses different hardware (GPUs, TPUs) and workloads; HPL scores are an imperfect proxy.

Data Schema

All live metrics are stored in versioned JSON files under data/. The current schema:

dimensionWhich of the six dimensions this data belongs to
metric_keySpecific metric identifier within that dimension
fetched_atISO 8601 UTC timestamp of when the data was fetched
window_daysRolling time window in days
source.nameHuman-readable source name
source.urlURL of the data source or API endpoint
summaryAggregated counts by country (US, China, Other, Unknown, total)
modelsArray of individual model records with lab, country, and timestamp
methodology_notePlain-language description of classification logic and caveats

Automation

Live data is refreshed daily by a GitHub Actions workflow (.github/workflows/update_frontier_models.yml). The workflow runs the Python fetch script, commits the updated JSON to the repo, and Vercel automatically re-deploys the site with the new data.

A manual run can be triggered at any time from the GitHub Actions tab using the workflow_dispatch trigger.

Static Scores

The radar chart and dimension scores on the main dashboard are manually assessed, analytical scores — not derived from a single data source. They represent a qualitative comparative judgment across the six dimensions, drawing on publicly available evidence. They are updated periodically as conditions change, not automatically.

Confidence designations (High / Medium / Lower) reflect the quality and availability of public data for each dimension, not the degree of U.S.–China divergence.

Coming Next

Future dimensions will follow the same pattern: one clear public data source, one v1 metric, transparent classification, automated refresh. Planned additions:

  • Compute: GPU shipment data, cloud capacity estimates, data center construction announcements
  • Talent: AI paper authorship by country (arXiv/Semantic Scholar), top-conference representation
  • Domestic Adoption: AI startup density, enterprise AI deployment surveys, government AI procurement
  • Global Diffusion: International model adoption, cloud infrastructure deployment by region
  • Energy: Data center power capacity, grid expansion data, renewable energy commitments

All data is sourced from publicly available APIs and datasets. No classified or proprietary information is used. Source code and data files are available in the project GitHub repository.
