Binding Constraints  —  Structural Bottlenecks

🇺🇸 U.S. Bottlenecks
  • Grid Capacity & Interconnection Delays Multi-year interconnection queues and aging transmission infrastructure are constraining large-scale data center buildout across key U.S. regions.
  • Energy Scaling Constraints Electricity demand from AI workloads is outpacing generation investment across major U.S. grids, with critical shortfalls projected through 2027.
  • Immigration & Talent Retention Risks H-1B uncertainty and visa processing backlogs create structural friction in recruiting and retaining international AI researchers and engineers.
🇨🇳 China Bottlenecks
  • Advanced Semiconductor Access Restricted access to sub-7nm logic and high-bandwidth memory materially limits the scale and capability of frontier training clusters.
  • Export Controls on Cutting-Edge GPUs BIS regulations on H100/H200-class hardware and derivatives cap training cluster scale and throttle access to leading AI accelerators.
  • Geopolitical Supply Chain Exposure Dependence on Taiwan-fabricated semiconductors and Dutch lithography equipment creates a durable structural vulnerability to external shock.

Near-Term Outlook  —  1–3 Year Swing Factors

Factor 01
Export Control Tightening or Loosening
Further BIS restrictions would deepen China's compute deficit and widen the U.S. frontier lead. Partial rollbacks or new licensing carve-outs could materially narrow the gap within 12–18 months.
↑ U.S. if tightened ↑ China if loosened
Factor 02
Breakthrough in Model Efficiency
A step-change in compute efficiency — architectural or training-method breakthroughs — disproportionately benefits the compute-constrained actor. Historically favors the trailing party.
↑ China benefit Contested
Factor 03
Major Power Infrastructure Acceleration
Federal permitting reform, accelerated SMR deployment, or high-voltage DC grid expansion would directly resolve the United States' primary AI scaling bottleneck.
↑ U.S. benefit
